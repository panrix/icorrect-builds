## The Assessment

He's a good tech who's checked out.

The quality of his work when he's actually doing it is solid — 10.4% QC rate is middle of the pack, his completions per hour worked are fine, and zero BM returns. The skill is there. This isn't someone who can't do the job.

But the pattern is damning. 10 lateness messages in 8 weeks — in his own words, from his own Slack messages. Ricky addressed it on 2 Feb, and within 4 days he was late twice more. By 26 Feb he's messaging Ricky the night before saying he's drunk. By 28 Feb he's on Instagram mid-repair on a Saturday, completing 2 of 8 items and leaving early. That's not someone who forgot to set an alarm — that's someone who's decided the consequences aren't real.

The 21% output drop is real but the meetings muddy it. The baseline (4.3/day) had no daily meetings. He's losing ~1h/day to meetings now, which takes the realistic target to about 4.0. He's at 3.4. That remaining 0.6/day gap is the late starts and the drift — about £44k/year in revenue not captured.

What concerns me most isn't the numbers — it's the trajectory. January: occasional lateness. February: escalating lateness, already addressed with no change, drunk message, Instagram on CCTV. The self-correction didn't hold. Each week is slightly worse than the last, not better. People who are going through a rough patch usually stabilise. People who are disengaging accelerate.

**Note:** The original audit claimed "1 written update in 3 weeks" and "zero proactive communication" — this was a methodology error. A follow-up query of item update replies found **83 replies in 3 weeks (4.9/day)**. He IS communicating on his repairs. The core problem is lateness and attendance, not communication.

The leadership path question is the real one. As a technician doing 3.4/day with decent quality, he's okay. Not great, not terrible. But he's positioned as a future leader who supervises Andreas and sets the standard for the refurb room. On punctuality and reliability — the visible, standard-setting behaviours — he's failing. He's the person arriving latest in the building.

He'll likely say the right things in the conversation. The question is whether behaviour changes by 16 March. The data package is strong enough that he can't argue with it — it's his own Slack messages, system timestamps, and CCTV. If he doesn't change after seeing all of that, he's telling you the answer.
