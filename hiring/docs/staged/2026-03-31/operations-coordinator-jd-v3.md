## About iCorrect

Every year, millions of Apple devices are written off as unrepairable. We exist to change that.

iCorrect is a specialist Apple repair operation based in London, built on a simple belief: if a device can be saved, it should be. We repair what Apple and everyone else turns away. Board-level diagnostics, microsoldering, screen refurbishment techniques we developed in-house. Every device we bring back to life is one less in a landfill.

We are not a big corporate operation. We are a small team of specialists who built this through passion, skill, and hard work. Our technicians do not swap parts and hope for the best. They understand the hardware at component level and push the boundaries of what is considered repairable. Customers send devices to us from across the country because nobody else will take them on.

Alongside our repair work, we run a buyback operation: purchasing, refurbishing, and reselling pre-owned Apple devices through certified platforms. It is another way we keep good hardware in circulation instead of in a drawer or a skip.

We are growing, the standards are high, and we are looking for people who share the belief that excellent repair work matters. For our customers and for the planet.

## The Role

We are looking for a Technical Operations Coordinator to be the operational heartbeat of our workshop. You will be the first face our clients see, the person who keeps every device tracked from intake to collection, and the technical bridge between our customers and our repair team.

This is not a general admin role. You need to understand Apple hardware well enough to assess a device at intake, identify models on sight, ask the right diagnostic questions, and communicate repair outcomes to customers with confidence. You are not on the bench doing repairs, but you need to understand what happens on the bench and why.

On any given day, you could be triaging a walk-in MacBook Pro with liquid damage, verifying specs on a batch of buyback devices, chasing a parts delivery, explaining a logic board diagnosis to a customer over the phone, and coordinating outbound shipments. All before lunch.

You need to be someone who thrives in that environment. Not someone who survives it.

## What You Will Do

- **Device intake and triage:** greet walk-in customers, identify the device model, check serial numbers, note visible damage, record the reported fault accurately, and set clear expectations on timelines and pricing so the technician has full context before they begin
- **Technical assessment:** perform initial device checks at intake, including verifying model, chip type, storage configuration, and condition. You are the first quality gate before a device reaches the bench
- **Buyback verification:** receive pre-owned Apple devices purchased through certified platforms, verify specifications against order details (model, chip, storage, condition grade), flag any discrepancies, coordinate diagnostic queues with the tech team, and ensure platform KPIs and deadlines are met
- **Customer communication:** answer calls and emails, translate technical repair updates into language customers understand, handle queries with confidence, and escalate only when a situation genuinely requires it
- **Shipping and logistics:** manage all inbound and outbound packages, including buyback orders, customer mail-ins, and completed repair dispatches via Royal Mail and couriers
- **Workshop coordination:** liaise with technicians on repair priorities, parts availability, and turnaround commitments. Understand enough about the repair process to prioritise intelligently, not just pass messages
- **Stock and parts:** receive parts shipments, verify contents against orders, and keep inventory records accurate
- **System management:** keep our project management platform updated so that every device in the building has a clear, current status at all times

## Who You Are

**You are technically minded.** You are the kind of person who checks a serial number rather than taking someone's word for it. You want to learn the difference between a display assembly and a display panel, between a storage fault and a logic board failure. You do not need to be a repair technician, but you want to understand what the technicians are doing and why. When something technical comes up, your instinct is to learn it, not avoid it.

**You are an Apple enthusiast.** You know your M-series chips from your Intel models. You can tell a MacBook Air from a MacBook Pro without flipping it over. You follow product launches, you understand Apple's lineup, and you care about the devices we work on. Not because you have to, but because you genuinely find it interesting.

**You take ownership.** When something needs doing, you do it. You do not wait to be told and you do not assume someone else will handle it. If a customer is waiting, a package is sitting unopened, or a status has not been updated, that is your responsibility and you feel it.

**You think before you escalate.** When a problem lands on your desk, your first instinct is to solve it. When you do need to escalate, you come with context, options, and a recommendation, not just a forwarded message.

**You communicate with precision.** Whether it is explaining a repair outcome to a customer, briefing a technician, or writing a status update for leadership, your communication is clear, concise, and technically accurate. Nothing important gets lost.

**You thrive under pressure.** A queue of walk-ins, a ringing phone, and a shipment deadline happening simultaneously is not chaos to you. It is a normal Tuesday. You prioritise, stay composed, and get through it.

## Application Questions

**1. Apple knowledge**
What Apple devices do you currently own or use daily? Pick one and tell us something specific you appreciate about how it is designed or built. Not just that you like it, but why.

**2. Technical thinking**
A customer brings in a MacBook Pro and says it keeps shutting down randomly. Before the device goes to a technician, what questions would you ask the customer to help narrow down the issue?

**3. Attention to detail**
You are verifying a buyback device against its order. The order says MacBook Pro 14-inch, M1 Pro, 16GB RAM, 512GB storage. You check the serial number and it comes back as an M1 Pro with 16GB RAM but 1TB storage. The device looks correct in every other way. What do you do?

**4. Initiative under pressure**
You are working the front desk alone. A walk-in customer arrives with a MacBook that will not turn on. While you are logging their device, the phone rings. It is another customer chasing an urgent repair update. At the same time, you notice a courier has just left three packages at the door that need to be checked in. Talk us through exactly how you handle the next ten minutes, step by step.

**5. Problem-solving without hand-holding**
A customer emails saying their device was returned to them after repair but the original issue is still present. You check the system and the repair was marked as complete by the technician two days ago. Your manager is not available. What do you do? Walk us through your thinking and your actions.

**6. Communication skills**
A customer drops off their MacBook for a screen repair. You know the repair typically takes 3 to 5 working days, but the team is currently backed up and it will likely take 7 to 8 days. The customer asks for a timeline. Write out exactly what you would say to them, word for word.

**7. Spec verification**
A customer brings in what they say is a MacBook Pro M2 with 16GB RAM. You check the serial number and it shows a MacBook Pro M1 with 8GB RAM. The customer insists they were told it was an M2 when they bought it. How do you handle this conversation?

**8. Self-awareness**
What is a mistake you made in a previous role that affected a customer or a team? What happened, what did you do about it, and what did you change afterwards?

**9. Availability**
Are you able to work on-site in central London five days a week? When would you be available to start?
